üéØ Real-World Example

Scenario: A startup company needs to launch a mobile app to compete in a rapidly evolving market.

üîÑ Agile Methods

üìú Historical Context

Dissatisfaction with the overheads involved in software design methods of the 1980s and 1990s led to the creation of agile methods. These traditional methods were often:

  • Too documentation-heavy
  • Slow to respond to changes
  • Focused on process over people
  • Resulted in excessive rework when requirements changed

üéØ What Are Agile Methods?

üìâ

Reduce Overheads

Aim to reduce overheads in the software process (e.g., by limiting documentation) and respond quickly to changing requirements without excessive rework

üöÄ

Deliver Quickly

Intended to deliver working software quickly and evolve this quickly to meet changing requirements

üíª

Focus on Code

Focus on the code rather than the design - working software is the primary measure of progress

üîÑ

Iterative Approach

Based on an iterative approach to software development with frequent releases and continuous feedback

‚ú® The Agile Philosophy

Agile methods represent a fundamental shift in thinking about software development:

  • From: Comprehensive documentation ‚Üí To: Working software
  • From: Following a plan ‚Üí To: Responding to change
  • From: Contract negotiation ‚Üí To: Customer collaboration
  • From: Processes and tools ‚Üí To: Individuals and interactions

üåü Popular Agile Methodologies

üé∏ Extreme Programming (XP) ‚ñº

Extreme Programming is perhaps the best-known and most widely used agile method. It takes an "extreme" approach to iterative development with:

  • New versions built several times per day
  • Increments delivered to customers every 2 weeks
  • All tests must run successfully for each build
  • Pair programming and collective code ownership
üèÉ Scrum ‚ñº

Scrum is a framework for managing agile projects with:

  • Short development cycles called "sprints" (typically 2-4 weeks)
  • Daily stand-up meetings
  • Product backlog managed by Product Owner
  • Sprint reviews and retrospectives
üè≠ Kanban ‚ñº

Kanban is a visual approach to managing work with:

  • Visual board showing work in different stages
  • Work-in-progress (WIP) limits
  • Continuous flow rather than fixed iterations
  • Focus on optimizing the flow of work
üíé Crystal ‚ñº

Crystal is a family of methodologies that emphasizes:

  • People and interactions over processes
  • Different "colors" for different project sizes
  • Frequent delivery and reflective improvement
  • Close communication and safety in the team

üìú The Agile Manifesto

üìÖ Historical Background

In February 2001, seventeen software developers met at a ski resort in Utah to discuss lightweight development methods. They published the Manifesto for Agile Software Development, which has since become the foundation for agile software development worldwide.

The Agile Manifesto

"We are uncovering better ways of developing software by doing it and helping others do it. Through this work we have come to value:"

Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
Working software over comprehensive documentation
Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
Responding to change over following a plan

"That is, while there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more."

üîç Understanding Each Value

üë• Individuals and Interactions over Processes and Tools

What it means: While processes and tools are important, the people doing the work and how they communicate are more critical to success.

Example: A team with excellent communication using simple tools will outperform a dysfunctional team with the best tools available.

üíª Working Software over Comprehensive Documentation

What it means: The primary measure of progress is working software. Documentation has value, but not if it comes at the expense of delivering working features.

Example: Instead of spending weeks writing detailed design documents before coding, agile teams start coding early and document what's actually built.

ü§ù Customer Collaboration over Contract Negotiation

What it means: Work with customers as partners rather than adversaries. Collaborate continuously rather than negotiate once and follow a fixed contract.

Example: Regular customer demos and feedback sessions throughout development, rather than showing them the final product after months of work.

üîÑ Responding to Change over Following a Plan

What it means: Plans are useful, but the ability to respond to change is more valuable. Adapt to new information rather than rigidly following an outdated plan.

Example: When market conditions change or new user needs emerge, agile teams can pivot quickly rather than insisting on completing the original plan.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Clarification

"While there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the left more."

This means agile doesn't reject processes, tools, documentation, contracts, or plans. It simply prioritizes the items on the left when trade-offs must be made.

üí° The Principles of Agile Methods

üéØ Core Agile Principles

Beyond the four values in the manifesto, agile methods are based on several key principles that guide how teams work and deliver software.

Principle Description
ü§ù Customer Involvement Customers should be closely involved throughout the development process. Their role is to provide and prioritize new system requirements and to evaluate the iterations of the system.
üì¶ Incremental Delivery The software is developed in increments with the customer specifying the requirements to be included in each increment.
üë• People not Process The skills of the development team should be recognized and exploited. Team members should be left to develop their own ways of working without prescriptive processes.
üîÑ Embrace Change Expect the system requirements to change and so design the system to accommodate these changes.
‚ú® Maintain Simplicity Focus on simplicity in both the software being developed and in the development process. Wherever possible, actively work to eliminate complexity from the system.

üîç Deep Dive into Each Principle

1. ü§ù Customer Involvement

The Principle: Customers should be closely involved throughout the development process.

Their Role:

  • Provide new system requirements
  • Prioritize features and requirements
  • Evaluate iterations of the system
  • Give continuous feedback
üí° In Practice

Example: In an XP project, the customer is actually part of the development team, sitting with developers and being available to answer questions and make decisions immediately.

2. üì¶ Incremental Delivery

The Principle: Software is developed in increments with the customer specifying requirements for each increment.

Key Characteristics:

  • Early increments serve as prototypes for later increments
  • Each increment adds new functionality
  • Customer gets working software early and often
  • Easier to get customer feedback and make changes
üí° In Practice

Example: Instead of delivering a complete e-commerce system after 12 months, deliver:

  • Month 1: Basic product catalog
  • Month 2: Shopping cart functionality
  • Month 3: Checkout and payment
  • Month 4: User accounts and order history

3. üë• People not Process

The Principle: Recognize and exploit the skills of the development team. Let team members develop their own ways of working.

Key Ideas:

  • Trust the team to self-organize
  • Don't impose rigid processes
  • Recognize that people are not interchangeable
  • Build projects around motivated individuals
üí° In Practice

Example: Rather than mandating that all teams follow the exact same process, allow each team to adapt practices to their specific context and skills. One team might do more pair programming, while another prefers code reviews.

4. üîÑ Embrace Change

The Principle: Expect system requirements to change and design the system to accommodate changes.

Why This Matters:

  • Business needs evolve constantly
  • Users discover what they really need through using the software
  • Technology and market conditions change
  • Competitors release new features
üí° In Practice

Example: When COVID-19 hit, many businesses needed to rapidly add features like contactless delivery, virtual consultations, or capacity management. Agile teams could pivot quickly to address these urgent needs.

5. ‚ú® Maintain Simplicity

The Principle: Focus on simplicity in both the software and the development process. Actively work to eliminate complexity.

How to Achieve This:

  • Build only what's needed now (YAGNI - You Aren't Gonna Need It)
  • Refactor code continuously to keep it clean
  • Use simple designs that can evolve
  • Avoid over-engineering and premature optimization
üí° In Practice

Example: Instead of building a complex, configurable system that might handle future needs, build the simplest thing that works today. Add complexity only when actually needed.

ü§î Deciding on the Balance

‚öñÔ∏è The Reality

Most projects include elements of BOTH plan-driven and agile processes. The key is finding the right balance for your context.

‚ùì Key Questions to Consider

1. Is detailed specification needed before implementation? ‚ñº

If YES ‚Üí Consider Plan-Driven

Examples where this applies:

  • Systems with strict regulatory requirements
  • Safety-critical systems (medical devices, aircraft controls)
  • Systems with complex interfaces to other systems
  • Projects where detailed contracts are required
2. Is incremental delivery realistic? ‚ñº

If YES ‚Üí Consider Agile

Incremental delivery works well when:

  • Customers can actively participate
  • Early feedback is valuable
  • Requirements are likely to change
  • System can be decomposed into independent features
3. What type of system is being developed? ‚ñº

Consider:

  • Plan-driven may be better for: Real-time systems with complex timing requirements, systems requiring extensive analysis before implementation
  • Agile may be better for: Web applications, mobile apps, business software where requirements evolve
4. What is the expected system lifetime? ‚ñº

Long-lifetime systems may require more design documentation to communicate original intentions to future support teams.

Short-lifetime systems can rely more on working code and minimal documentation.

5. What technologies are available? ‚ñº

Agile methods rely on good tools to keep track of an evolving design:

  • Version control systems
  • Automated testing frameworks
  • Continuous integration tools
  • Collaboration platforms
6. How is the development team organized? ‚ñº

Distributed teams or outsourced development may need more detailed design documents to communicate across teams.

Co-located teams can rely more on face-to-face communication and lightweight documentation.

7. Are there cultural or organizational constraints? ‚ñº

Traditional engineering organizations may have a culture of plan-based development. Cultural change takes time and may need to happen gradually.

8. How skilled are the designers and programmers? ‚ñº

Agile methods are sometimes argued to require higher skill levels than plan-based approaches where programmers translate detailed designs into code.

However, successful agile teams invest heavily in continuous learning and skill development.

9. Is the system subject to external regulation? ‚ñº

If a system must be approved by external regulators (e.g., FDA for medical software, FAA for aviation software), you will likely need detailed documentation as part of the system safety case.

However, even regulated systems can use agile practices with appropriate documentation.

üìö Requirements as User Stories

üé≠ What are User Stories?

In XP, requirements are expressed as scenarios (also known as user stories) that are implemented as a series of tasks.

  • Written on story cards by customers
  • Development team breaks them into implementation tasks
  • These tasks form the basis of schedule and cost estimates
  • Customer prioritizes stories for each release
üìù Example User Stories
Story 1: Patient Search

As a doctor, I want to search for patients by name or ID so that I can quickly access their medical records.

Story 2: Appointment Scheduling

As a receptionist, I want to book appointments for patients so that doctors know who to expect each day.

Story 3: Prescription Generation

As a doctor, I want to generate electronic prescriptions so that patients can get their medications from any pharmacy.

‚ö†Ô∏è XP and Change

Conventional wisdom: Design for change. Spend time anticipating changes to reduce costs later.

XP philosophy: Changes cannot be reliably anticipated. Instead, use constant code improvement (refactoring) to make changes easier when they need to be implemented.

üõ†Ô∏è Extreme Programming Practices

üéØ Overview

XP is characterized by a set of specific practices that work together to support rapid, high-quality software development. These practices are designed to be mutually reinforcing.

üìã XP Practices (Part A)

Practice Description
üìÖ Incremental Planning Requirements are recorded on story cards and the stories to be included in a release are determined by the time available and their relative priority. The developers break these stories into development 'Tasks'.
üì¶ Small Releases The minimal useful set of functionality that provides business value is developed first. Releases of the system are frequent and incrementally add functionality to the first release.
‚ú® Simple Design Enough design is carried out to meet the current requirements and no more.
üß™ Test-First Development An automated unit test framework is used to write tests for a new piece of functionality before that functionality itself is implemented.
üîÑ Refactoring All developers are expected to refactor the code continuously as soon as possible code improvements are found. This keeps the code simple and maintainable.

üìã XP Practices (Part B)

Practice Description
üë• Pair Programming Developers work in pairs, checking each other's work and providing the support to always do a good job.
ü§ù Collective Ownership The pairs of developers work on all areas of the system, so that no islands of expertise develop and all the developers take responsibility for all of the code. Anyone can change anything.
üîó Continuous Integration As soon as the work on a task is complete, it is integrated into the whole system. After any such integration, all the unit tests in the system must pass.
‚öñÔ∏è Sustainable Pace Large amounts of overtime are not considered acceptable as the net effect is often to reduce code quality and medium term productivity.
üè¢ On-Site Customer A representative of the end-user of the system (the customer) should be available full time for the use of the XP team. In an extreme programming process, the customer is a member of the development team and is responsible for bringing system requirements to the team for implementation.

üîç Deep Dive into Key Practices

‚ú® Simple Design

Philosophy: Do the simplest thing that could possibly work.

Key principles:

  • Design for today's requirements, not tomorrow's
  • Don't add functionality until it's needed (YAGNI - You Aren't Gonna Need It)
  • Choose the simplest design that meets current needs
  • Trust that refactoring will allow you to adapt to future needs
üí° Example

‚ùå Not Simple: Building a complex, configurable system that might handle future requirements you've imagined.

‚úÖ Simple: Building exactly what's needed now, knowing you can refactor later if requirements change.

üîÑ Refactoring

What is it: Improving the internal structure of code without changing its external behavior.

Why it's essential:

  • Keeps code clean and maintainable
  • Makes future changes easier
  • Reduces technical debt
  • Improves code understandability
üîß Examples of Refactoring
1. Re-organization of Class Hierarchy

Removing duplicate code by extracting common functionality into parent classes

2. Tidying and Renaming

Renaming attributes and methods to make them easier to understand

3. Code Extraction

Replacing inline code with calls to methods that have been included in a program library

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Note

Some changes require architecture refactoring, which is much more expensive and risky than code-level refactoring.

ü§ù Collective Ownership

Philosophy: The code belongs to the team, not to individuals.

Benefits:

  • No knowledge silos - multiple people understand each part of the system
  • Reduced risk when team members leave
  • Any pair can work on any part of the system
  • Encourages code consistency and quality

Key principle: "Anyone can change anything."

üîó Continuous Integration

Practice: Integrate code into the main codebase multiple times per day.

Process:

  1. Developer completes a task
  2. Code is integrated into the whole system immediately
  3. All unit tests are run automatically
  4. Build is only accepted if all tests pass

Benefits:

  • Integration problems are detected early
  • Reduces integration hell at the end of projects
  • Always have a working version of the system
  • Encourages developers to write tests

‚öñÔ∏è Sustainable Pace

Philosophy: Software development is a marathon, not a sprint.

Key principle: Large amounts of overtime are not considered acceptable because:

  • Tired developers make more mistakes
  • Code quality decreases
  • Medium-term productivity drops
  • Team burnout leads to turnover

XP Guideline: Teams should work at a pace they can sustain indefinitely (typically 40-hour work weeks).

üè¢ On-Site Customer

Practice: A customer representative is a full-time member of the development team.

Responsibilities:

  • Provide and clarify requirements
  • Prioritize features
  • Answer questions immediately
  • Make decisions about trade-offs
  • Accept or reject completed work

Challenge: In practice, it's often difficult to get a customer to commit full-time to the project.

Testing is central to XP and XP has developed an approach where the program is tested after every change has been made.

üèóÔ∏è Test-First Development

The Process

  1. Write a test for the next bit of functionality you want to add
  2. Run the test - it should fail (since the functionality doesn't exist yet)
  3. Write the simplest code that makes the test pass
  4. Run all tests to ensure nothing broke
  5. Refactor the code to improve its structure
  6. Repeat for the next piece of functionality

‚úÖ Benefits of Test-First Development

  • Clarifies requirements: Writing tests forces you to understand what the code should do
  • Ensures testability: Code is designed to be testable from the start
  • Provides documentation: Tests serve as examples of how to use the code
  • Reduces debugging time: Problems are caught immediately
  • Builds confidence: Comprehensive test suite allows refactoring without fear

ü§ù Customer Involvement in Testing

The Customer's Role

The customer helps develop acceptance tests for the stories that are to be implemented in the next release.

Process:

  1. Customer writes acceptance criteria for user stories
  2. Development team automates these tests
  3. All new code is validated against acceptance tests
  4. Ensures system meets customer needs

  1. A number in mg representing a single dose of the drug
  2. A number representing the number of single doses per day

Tests:

  1. Test for inputs where the single dose is correct but the frequency is too high
  2. Test for inputs where the single dose is too high and too low
  3. Test for inputs where single dose √ó frequency is too high and too low
  4. Test for inputs where single dose √ó frequency is in the permitted range

Output:

OK or error message indicating that the dose is outside the safe range

‚ö†Ô∏è Challenge with Customer Involvement

People adopting the customer role have limited time available and cannot work full-time with the development team. They may feel that providing requirements was enough and be reluctant to get involved in testing.

ü§ñ Test Automation

Why Automate?

Tests are written as executable programs rather than data so they can be executed automatically.

Key characteristics:

  • Tests are stand-alone programs
  • They simulate input submission
  • They check that results meet specifications
  • Automated test frameworks (e.g., JUnit) make this easy

Benefits of Automation

Since testing is automated, there is always a set of tests that can be quickly and easily executed:

  • Run all tests whenever any functionality is added
  • Catch problems introduced by new code immediately
  • Build confidence in the codebase
  • Enable fearless refactoring

‚ö†Ô∏è XP Testing Difficulties

Common Challenges

  • Developer Preference: Programmers prefer programming to testing and sometimes take shortcuts when writing tests. They may write incomplete tests that don't check all possible exceptions.
  • Difficult to Write Incrementally: Some tests are very difficult to write incrementally. For example, in complex user interfaces, it's often difficult to write unit tests for display logic and workflow between screens.
  • Completeness Uncertainty: It's difficult to judge the completeness of a set of tests. Although you may have many system tests, your test set may not provide complete coverage.

ü§ù What is Pair Programming?

In XP, programmers work in pairs, sitting together to develop code. This practice is one of the most distinctive and sometimes controversial aspects of XP.

üéØ How Pair Programming Works

‚å®Ô∏è

The Driver

Writes the code, thinking about the immediate task at hand - syntax, implementation details, current test

üîç

The Navigator

Reviews the code as it's written, thinks about the bigger picture - design, potential issues, next steps

üîÑ Dynamic Pairing

  • Programmers sit together at the same workstation
  • Pairs are created dynamically so all team members work with each other
  • Roles (driver/navigator) switch frequently
  • No fixed pairing assignments - promotes knowledge sharing

‚ú® Benefits of Pair Programming

1. üß† Collective Code Ownership

Pair programming helps develop common ownership of code and spreads knowledge across the team.

  • Multiple people understand each part of the system
  • Reduces "bus factor" (risk of knowledge loss)
  • No single points of failure in knowledge

2. üëÄ Informal Code Review

Serves as an informal review process as each line of code is looked at by more than one person.

  • Catches errors immediately
  • Improves code quality
  • Encourages better naming and structure
  • No need for separate code review meetings

3. üîÑ Encourages Refactoring

It encourages refactoring as the whole team can benefit from improvements.

  • Both programmers see opportunities for improvement
  • One can refactor while other ensures tests still pass
  • Shared understanding makes refactoring safer

4. üéì Knowledge Transfer

The sharing of knowledge that happens during pair programming is very important.

  • Junior developers learn from senior developers
  • Senior developers learn new perspectives
  • Domain knowledge spreads across the team
  • Technical skills are shared

5. üéØ Better Focus and Discipline

  • Less likely to get distracted
  • Social pressure to stay on task
  • Encourages following good practices
  • Both programmers keep each other motivated

üìä Productivity Research

üî¨ Research Findings

Measurements suggest that development productivity with pair programming is similar to that of two people working independently.

However, pair programming produces:

  • Higher quality code (fewer defects)
  • Better design decisions
  • More maintainable code
  • Shared knowledge across the team

Conclusion: While you might write similar amounts of code, the quality is significantly higher, leading to fewer bugs and less rework.

üé® Advantages Summary

‚úÖ Collective Ownership & Responsibility

Supports the idea of collective ownership and responsibility for the system. Individuals are not held responsible for problems with the code. Instead, the team has collective responsibility for resolving problems.

‚úÖ Informal Review Process

Acts as an informal review process because each line of code is looked at by at least two people.

‚úÖ Supports Refactoring

Helps support refactoring, which is a process of software improvement. Where pair programming and collective ownership are used, others benefit immediately from the refactoring so they are likely to support the process.

‚ö†Ô∏è Challenges and Considerations

Potential Challenges

  • Personality Conflicts: Not everyone works well together; need to manage pair dynamics
  • Skill Imbalances: Large skill gaps can lead to one person dominating
  • Fatigue: Pairing requires intense focus and can be mentally exhausting
  • Space Requirements: Requires appropriate workspace setup
  • Initial Resistance: Some developers resist the practice initially

üí° Best Practices for Successful Pair Programming

  • Switch driver/navigator roles frequently (every 15-30 minutes)
  • Communicate openly and respectfully
  • Take regular breaks
  • Rotate pairs regularly
  • Ensure comfortable workspace with large monitor
  • Start with simple tasks when learning the practice

üìä Agile Project Management

üéØ The Challenge

The principal responsibility of software project managers is to manage the project so that software is delivered on time and within the planned budget.

üìã

Plan-Driven Management

Managers draw up a plan showing what should be delivered, when, and who will work on development

üîÑ

Agile Management

Requires a different approach adapted to incremental development and the particular strengths of agile methods

üîÑ Key Differences in Agile Project Management

1. üéØ Planning Approach

Traditional: Detailed upfront planning for the entire project

Agile: High-level planning with detailed planning only for the current iteration

Agile Planning Levels:

  • Release Planning: What features will be in the next release?
  • Iteration Planning: What tasks will we complete in this iteration?
  • Daily Planning: What will each person work on today?

2. üìè Progress Measurement

Traditional: Measure progress against the plan (percentage complete)

Agile: Measure progress by working software delivered

Agile Metrics:

  • Velocity: How many story points completed per iteration?
  • Burn-down Charts: How much work remains?
  • Working Software: What features are actually done?

3. ü§ù Team Management

Traditional: Manager assigns tasks to team members

Agile: Self-organizing teams decide who works on what

  • Team members volunteer for tasks
  • Team collectively commits to iteration goals
  • Manager facilitates rather than directs

4. üìä Reporting and Communication

Traditional: Formal status reports and meetings

Agile: Daily stand-ups and visible progress boards

  • Short daily meetings (15 minutes)
  • Each person answers: What did I do? What will I do? Any blockers?
  • Information radiators (visible boards showing progress)

üåü The Scrum Master Role

In Scrum (a popular agile framework), the project manager role is similar to a Scrum Master:

  • Facilitates rather than directs
  • Removes impediments blocking the team
  • Protects the team from external interference
  • Coaches the team on agile practices
  • Ensures the process is followed

üìà Scaling Agile Methods

‚úÖ Where Agile Works Best

Agile methods have proved to be successful for small and medium-sized projects that can be developed by a small co-located team.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Scaling Challenge

With the focus on small, tightly-integrated teams, there are problems in scaling agile methods to large systems.

üöß Problems with Scaling Agile

1. üë• Team Size and Communication

Problem: Agile methods are most effective when the system can be developed with a small co-located team who can communicate informally.

Challenge for Large Systems:

  • Large systems require larger development teams
  • Teams may be distributed across locations or time zones
  • Informal communication becomes difficult
  • Coordination overhead increases significantly

2. üîó System Complexity and Integration

Problem: Large systems are usually collections of separate, communicating systems.

Challenges:

  • Many requirements concern system interactions
  • Requirements don't lend themselves to flexibility and incremental development
  • Significant fraction of development is system configuration rather than new code
  • Integration becomes a major undertaking

3. üìú External Regulations and Standards

Problem: Large systems and their development processes are often constrained by external rules and regulations.

Impact:

  • May require comprehensive documentation
  • Need to demonstrate compliance with standards
  • Limits flexibility in process and delivery
  • Approval processes may not align with rapid iterations

4. ‚è≥ Long Development Cycles

Problem: Large systems have long procurement and development times.

Challenges:

  • Difficult to maintain coherent teams over long periods
  • People inevitably move to other jobs and projects
  • Knowledge loss as team members leave
  • Team dynamics constantly changing

5. üë• Multiple Stakeholders

Problem: Large systems usually have a diverse set of stakeholders.

Challenge: It's practically impossible to involve all different stakeholders in the development process.

  • Conflicting priorities and requirements
  • Can't have all stakeholders as "on-site customers"
  • Difficult to get quick decisions
  • Political complexity increases

6. üè¢ Organizational and Cultural Issues

Problem: Team members may be unsuited to the intense involvement that characterizes agile methods.

Challenges:

  • Not everyone thrives in highly collaborative environments
  • Some organizational cultures resistant to change
  • Traditional project management offices may conflict with agile approaches
  • Contract structures may not support agile delivery

7. üìÑ Documentation Needs

Challenge: While agile emphasizes working software over documentation, large systems often need substantial documentation for:

  • Regulatory compliance
  • Long-term maintenance
  • Training new team members
  • Integration with other systems

üí° Strategies for Scaling Agile

1. üèóÔ∏è Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe)

A framework for applying agile at enterprise scale with:

  • Multiple teams coordinated through "Agile Release Trains"
  • Portfolio, program, and team levels
  • Synchronized iterations and planning
  • Architectural runway for technical foundation

2. üéØ LeSS (Large-Scale Scrum)

Applies Scrum principles to multiple teams working on one product:

  • One product backlog
  • One product owner
  • Multiple teams working in sync
  • Emphasis on simplicity

3. üåÄ Disciplined Agile Delivery (DAD)

A hybrid approach that provides guidance for scaling:

  • Combines practices from multiple agile methods
  • Provides decision framework
  • Acknowledges need for governance
  • Flexible based on context

4. üîß Practical Approaches

  • Scrum of Scrums: Representatives from each team meet regularly to coordinate
  • Communities of Practice: Specialists across teams share knowledge
  • Architectural Governance: Ensure technical consistency across teams
  • Shared Services: Common infrastructure and tools
  • Incremental Documentation: Create documentation as needed, not all upfront

‚öñÔ∏è The Bottom Line

üéØ Key Takeaway

Scaling agile is possible but challenging. Success requires:

  • Adapting agile principles to larger context
  • Additional coordination mechanisms
  • Accepting some increased overhead
  • Strong organizational support
  • Willingness to experiment and adapt

Remember: For large systems that require larger development teams, a plan-driven approach may have to be used, or a hybrid approach combining agile and plan-driven elements.
