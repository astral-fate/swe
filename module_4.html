Requirements Management is an ongoing activity that overlaps with all other stages to handle changes.

  • Elicitation and Analysis: Working with customers and stakeholders to find out about the application domain, the services needed, and the system's constraints.
  • Specification: The process of writing down the user and system requirements in a requirements document.
  • Validation: Checking that the documented requirements define the system the customer really wants.

Elicitation & Validation

Elicitation: Discovering What Stakeholders Really Want

Discovering requirements is challenging because stakeholders often don't know what they want, express requirements in their own terms, or have conflicting needs. Several techniques can help.

  • Interviewing: Formal or informal interviews with stakeholders to get an overall understanding of what they do and how they might interact with the system.
  • Scenarios & Use Cases: Real-life examples of how a system can be used. Use cases identify the actors in an interaction and describe the interaction itself.
  • Ethnography: A social scientist observes how people *actually* work, rather than how they say they work. This can reveal important social and organizational requirements.

Validation: Checking the Requirements

This is a critical step to ensure you're building the right system. Fixing an error at this stage is far cheaper than fixing it after the system is delivered.

  • Validity: Does the system provide the functions that best support the customer‚Äôs needs?
  • Consistency: Are there any conflicting requirements?
  • Completeness: Are all functions required by the customer included?
  • Realism: Can the requirements be implemented with the available budget and technology?
  • Verifiability: Can the requirements be tested?
