üìñ Comprehensive Glossary

Requirements Engineering ‚ñº

Requirements Engineering is the process of establishing the services that the customer requires from a system and the constraints under which it operates and is developed. The requirements themselves are the descriptions of the system services and constraints that are generated during the requirements engineering process.

User Requirements ‚ñº

User Requirements are statements in natural language plus diagrams of the services the system provides and its operational constraints. They are written for customers who may not have a technical background.

System Requirements ‚ñº

System Requirements are structured documents setting out detailed descriptions of the system's services and operational constraints. They define what should be implemented and may be part of a contract between client and contractor.

Functional Requirements ‚ñº

Functional Requirements are statements of services the system should provide, how the system should react to particular inputs, and how the system should behave in particular situations. They may also state what the system should NOT do.

Non-Functional Requirements ‚ñº

Non-Functional Requirements are constraints on the services or functions offered by the system such as timing constraints, constraints on the development process, standards, etc. They often apply to the system as a whole rather than individual features or services.

Domain Requirements ‚ñº

Domain Requirements are constraints on the system from the domain of operation. They reflect characteristics and constraints of the application domain.

Requirements Elicitation ‚ñº

Requirements Elicitation is the process of gathering information about the required and existing systems and distilling the user and system requirements from this information. It involves interacting with stakeholders to discover their requirements.

Requirements Validation ‚ñº

Requirements Validation is concerned with demonstrating that the requirements define the system that the customer really wants. It checks for validity, consistency, completeness, realism, and verifiability.

Requirements Management ‚ñº

Requirements Management is the process of managing changing requirements during the requirements engineering process and system development. It involves keeping track of individual requirements and maintaining links between dependent requirements.

Requirements Specification ‚ñº

Requirements Specification is the process of writing down the user and system requirements in a requirements document. It's crucial that this document is as complete as possible.

Stakeholder ‚ñº

Stakeholder is any person or organization who is affected by the system in some way and therefore has a legitimate interest in it. This includes end-users, managers, engineers, domain experts, and others.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Issues with Functional Requirements

1. Ambiguity

Ambiguous requirements may be interpreted in different ways by developers and users.

‚ùå Bad Example: "The system should be fast."

‚úÖ Good Example: "The system shall respond to user queries within 200 milliseconds for 95% of requests."

2. Imprecision

Requirements that are not precisely stated may cause problems.

‚ùå Bad Example: "The system should handle many users."

‚úÖ Good Example: "The system shall support at least 500 concurrent users."

3. Incompleteness & Inconsistency

In principle, requirements should be both complete and consistent:

  • Complete: Include descriptions of all facilities required
  • Consistent: No conflicts or contradictions

‚ö†Ô∏è Reality Check: In practice, it is impossible to produce a complete and consistent requirements document!

üèóÔ∏è Implementation Impact

üé≠ Requirements and Design

What is Requirements Validation?

The process of demonstrating that the requirements define the system that the customer really wants.

üéØ Types of Requirements Checking

1. ‚úÖ Validity

Does the system provide the functions which best support the customer's needs?

Question to ask: Are we building the right system for the customer?
2. üîÑ Consistency

Are there any requirements conflicts?

Example conflict: "The system must respond in 1 second" vs. "The system must perform complex data analysis" (may be impossible simultaneously)
3. üìã Completeness

Are all functions required by the customer included?

Check for: Missing requirements, incomplete definitions, missing constraints
4. üí° Realism

Can the requirements be implemented given available budget and technology?

Consider: Technical feasibility, cost constraints, time limitations, available expertise
5. ‚úì Verifiability

Can the requirements be checked?

Principle: Every requirement should be testable. If you can't test it, you can't verify it's been implemented correctly!

üéØ Key Requirements Management Activities

Each requirement must be uniquely identified so that it can be cross-referenced with other requirements.

üí° Example Identification Scheme:

  • NFR-PERF-001: Performance Non-Functional Requirement 1

2. Change Management Process

A set of activities that assess the impact and cost of changes.

üìã Change Management Stages:

Step 1: Problem Analysis & Change Specification

The problem or change proposal is analyzed to check that it is valid. This analysis is fed back to the change requestor who may respond with a more specific requirements change proposal, or decide to withdraw the request.

‚¨áÔ∏è
Step 2: Change Analysis & Costing

The effect of the proposed change is assessed using traceability information and general knowledge of the system requirements. Once this analysis is completed, a decision is made whether or not to proceed with the requirements change.

‚¨áÔ∏è
Step 3: Change Implementation

The requirements document and, where necessary, the system design and implementation, are modified. Ideally, the document should be organized so that changes can be easily implemented.

3. Traceability Policies

Define the relationships between requirements and between requirements and system design that should be recorded.

Types of Traceability:
Traceability Type Description
Source Traceability Links requirements to stakeholders who proposed them
Requirements Traceability Links dependent requirements
Design Traceability Links requirements to design components
